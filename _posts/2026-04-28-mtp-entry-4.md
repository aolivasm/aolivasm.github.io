In our previous entry, we looked at how **MTPs** can be used to validate biological markers, like antibody titers. Now, we are going to move from a biological dose to a clinical workflow. 

To do this, we are looking at a foundational 2013 paper by Sebastien Haneuse and Andrea Rotnitzky, which investigates a variable every surgeon and anesthesiologist obsesses over: **Operating Room (OR) Time.**

> **As a historical note:** This is the first study to refer to these specific types of interventions as **Modified Treatment Policies**.

### The Clinical Problem: Does Speed Save Lives?
The researchers wanted to answer a specific question regarding patients undergoing surgical resection for non-small-cell lung cancer (NSCLC): *Does reducing the time a patient spends in surgery causally reduce their risk of post-operative complications?*

If we apply standard causal inference methods to this problem, we run into an immediate logical wall. Standard methods define causal effects by comparing fixed targets. They ask questions like: *"What if we forced every single lung resection to take exactly 60 minutes?"*

To a clinician, this is nonsensical. Surgery length is heavily dictated by the patient's natural anatomy and the complexity of the tumor. 
* A 45-minute wedge resection on a healthy patient cannot be meaningfully compared to a 3-hour anatomic resection on a patient with heavy comorbidities. 
* Forcing a 3-hour surgery to conclude in 60 minutes wouldn't improve outcomes; it would likely result in catastrophic surgical errors. 

### The MTP Solution: The "Efficiency Shift"
Instead of an impossible fixed target, Haneuse and Rotnitzky used an MTP to evaluate an **efficiency shift**. 

They asked: *"What if we implemented a hospital-wide protocol that reduced every surgery’s natural duration by a realistic amount, say $\delta = 15$ minutes?"*

This approach respects clinical reality. It scales with complexity, allowing a 3-hour surgery to become a 2-hour-and-45-minute surgery, rather than forcing it into an impossible 60-minute window.

### The Clinical Insight
By applying this MTP framework, the researchers revealed a substantial clinical finding: for anatomic lung resections, a 15-minute reduction in operative time causally reduced the odds of a major complication by roughly 30%. 

This gives hospital administrators concrete, causal evidence to invest in systems-level efficiency protocols (like improved instrument staging, dedicated thoracic surgical teams, or optimized anesthesia induction) rather than just demanding arbitrary speed from the surgeon.

### Looking Ahead: The Positivity Boundary
In addition to naming the framework, Haneuse and Rotnitzky were the first to rigorously address a major mathematical and clinical hurdle in these types of shifts: **Positivity**. 

Even with a modest 15-minute shift, we inevitably hit a biological limit. A surgery cannot take zero minutes, nor can a complex resection safely bypass the absolute minimum time required to open and close a patient. Bounding these theoretical shifts so they remain within the realm of feasible reality is a critical concept—one we will dedicate **Entry 6** entirely to unpacking.

**Reference:**

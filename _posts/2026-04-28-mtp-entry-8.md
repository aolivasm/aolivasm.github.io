Over the last seven entries, we have built a powerful toolkit. We’ve learned how MTPs solve the problem of "impossible" static targets, and how strategies like the Tapered Shift can save our math from hitting the Positivity Wall. 

But causal inference is not just about making the math work. It is about making the math reflect medicine. In this entry, we step back and look at the three major clinical limitations of MTPs.

### Limitation 1: The "Moving Target" Problem
In Entry 7, we saw that to avoid positivity violations, we could either restrict the population or use a Tapered Shift. But we must be honest about the trade-off: **we are actively changing the question.**

If public health officials ask, "What is the effect of giving *everyone* a vaccine booster?" and we answer with a Tapered Shift (which gives full boosters to some and partial/zero boosters to others), we have shifted the target. While our new estimate is mathematically valid, we must always ask: *Is this mathematically valid answer still the clinical answer we need?*

### Limitation 2: The Translation Gap
Writing a policy like $d(A) = A - 15 \text{ minutes}$ or "decrease BMI by 5%" on a whiteboard is easy. Actually inventing a hospital protocol, a pill, or a behavioral intervention that executes that exact shift in the real world is incredibly hard. 

An MTP gives us a scientifically grounded *target* for population health. However, translating that target into an actionable, implementable clinical protocol is a hurdle that statistics cannot solve alone; it requires implementation science and clinical reality checks.

### Limitation 3: The Mechanism Trap (The CAST Trial)
Even if we could execute the perfect shift, we face one of the deepest assumptions in causal inference: **Consistency**. This assumption says that *how* a patient arrives at an exposure level doesn't matter, only the level itself. In observational studies, this is rarely true.

Consider the famous **Cardiac Arrhythmia Suppression Trial (CAST)** in the 1980s. Observational data showed a clear pattern: patients who had fewer arrhythmias after a heart attack had higher survival rates. The logical intervention? Give patients antiarrhythmic drugs to suppress their arrhythmias and mimic that healthy state. 

However, the CAST trial had to be stopped early because the patients receiving the antiarrhythmics had a *higher* mortality rate.

**The Lesson:** The *mechanism* of the shift matters. In the observational data, the absence of arrhythmias was merely a **marker** of a healthier, less damaged heart. The antiarrhythmic drugs didn't fix the underlying heart damage; they just artificially masked the symptom.

When we use an MTP to say, "What if we shifted everyone's immune markers up?", we are assuming that the mechanism we use to raise that number (like a vaccine booster) actually confers real biological defense, rather than just artificially inflating a lab value without fixing the underlying vulnerability. Causal math cannot distinguish between healing a patient and masking a symptom—that is the clinician's job.

Over the last seven entries, we have built a powerful toolkit. We’ve learned how MTPs solve the problem of "impossible" static targets, and how strategies like the Tapered Shift can save our math from hitting the Positivity Wall. 

But causal inference is not just about making the math work. It is about making the math reflect medicine. In this entry, we step back and look at the three major clinical limitations of MTPs.

### Limitation 1: The "Moving Target" Problem
In Entry 7, we saw that to avoid positivity violations, we could either restrict the population or use a Tapered Shift. But we must be honest about the trade-off: **we are actively changing the question.**

If public health officials ask, "What is the effect of giving *everyone* a vaccine booster?" and we answer with a Tapered Shift (which gives full boosters to some and partial/zero boosters to others), we have shifted the target. While our new estimate is mathematically valid, we must always ask: *Is this mathematically valid answer still the clinical answer we need?*

### Limitation 2: The Translation Gap
Writing a policy like "increase immune markers by 1 unit" or "decrease BMI by 5%" on a whiteboard is easy. Actually inventing a hospital protocol, a pill, or a behavioral intervention that executes that exact shift in the real world is incredibly hard. 

An MTP gives us a scientifically grounded *target* for population health. However, translating that target into an actionable, implementable clinical protocol is a hurdle that statistics cannot solve alone; it requires implementation science and clinical reality checks.

### Limitation 3: The Mechanism Trap (The CAST Trial)
Even if we could execute the perfect shift, we face a clinical hurdle related to a core concept in causal inference: **Consistency**. 

Consistency requires that if we intervene to force a patient to a certain exposure level, their outcome will be the same as a patient who arrived at that exposure level *naturally*. In observational studies, this is rarely true because there are often multiple, biologically distinct ways to arrive at the same number.

*(Note for the methodologists: We intentionally left strict individual-level consistency out of our "Three Rules" in Entry 6. MTPs rely on a powerful mathematical relaxation of this assumption, which you can explore in Olivas-Martinez, Gilbert, and Rotnitzky, 2025. However, the clinical warning below still severely applies).*

Consider the famous **Cardiac Arrhythmia Suppression Trial (CAST)** in the 1980s. In the observational studies that preceded the trial, the "exposure" being evaluated was the arrhythmia itself. The data clearly showed that patients who naturally had no arrhythmias after a heart attack had higher survival rates. 

The flawed logical leap was to use antiarrhythmic drugs as an *intervention* to force the "no arrhythmia" exposure state onto everyone. However, the CAST trial had to be stopped early because the patients receiving the drugs had a *higher* mortality rate.

**The Lesson:** This was a fatal violation of consistency. The clinical outcome of "No Arrhythmias (Natural Recovery)" was drastically different from "No Arrhythmias (Drug-Induced)." The drug suppressed the electrical symptom (forcing the exposure to zero), but it did not replicate the healthy myocardium that *naturally* prevents arrhythmias.

When we use an MTP to say, "What if we shifted everyone's immune markers up?", our math is manipulating the *exposure*. But as clinicians, we must ask: does the real-world *intervention* (like a vaccine booster) actually mimic the protective biology of someone who naturally has high immune markers? If the mechanism we use to shift the number introduces new toxicities or fails to address the underlying vulnerability, our mathematical shift is just a dangerous illusion.

Up to this point, our case studies have focused on simple, single-snapshot interventions applied to isolated patients. But medicine is rarely that simple. Chronic diseases evolve over time, and infectious diseases jump from person to person. 

Fortunately, the Modified Treatment Policy framework is actively evolving to handle these real-world complexities.

### 1. Handling Time: Longitudinal MTPs (LMTPs)
Most clinical exposures are not static one-time events; they are dynamic. Think of a patient adjusting their physical activity over months, or a clinician titrating blood pressure medications visit after visit. 

To handle this, methodologists have developed **Longitudinal Modified Treatment Policies (LMTPs)**. Instead of applying a shift at a single baseline point, an LMTP allows us to evaluate dynamic treatment regimes over time. We can estimate the effect of a policy that says: *"At every clinical visit over the next year, shift the patient's current medication dose down by 10% if their symptoms remain stable."* This allows for causal estimates that truly mimic the ongoing nature of chronic disease management.

### 2. Handling Networks: Interference and Spillover
Traditional statistics relies heavily on the "IID" assumption (Independent and Identically Distributed). Clinically, this means we assume that *my* treatment only affects *my* outcome.

However, in many fields of public health, this assumption is wildly false:
* **Infectious Diseases:** If a policy shifts your immune markers up via a vaccine, it doesn't just protect you; it provides herd immunity, lowering *my* risk of infection as well.
* **Environmental Health:** If a policy shifts a factory's air pollution output down by 10% in one city, the wind blows that benefit to the neighboring cities. 

This phenomenon is called **Interference** (or spillover). Methodologists like **Nima Hejazi and colleagues** are currently expanding MTPs to operate within networks. By accounting for spatial and social structures, these advanced network-MTPs allow us to estimate not just the direct effect of a shift on the individual, but the indirect, spillover effects on their community.

While the math behind LMTPs and Network Interference is highly complex, the core philosophy remains the same: we are building causal tools that bend to fit clinical reality, rather than forcing clinical reality to fit into rigid, impossible assumptions.

* Balkus, S. V., Delaney, S. W., & Hejazi, N. S. (2026). The causal effects of modified treatment policies under network interference. *Journal of the Royal Statistical Society Series B: Statistical Methodology*.

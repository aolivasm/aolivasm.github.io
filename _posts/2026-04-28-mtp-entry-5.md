In our final case study, we look at a landmark 2009 study by **Sarah Taubman and colleagues**. This work evaluated the 20-year risk of coronary heart disease (CHD) under various hypothetical lifestyle interventions within the famous **Nurses' Health Study**.

### The Clinical Problem: The "Impossible" Target
One of the most valuable lessons in this paper is the warning against "impossible" static targets. The researchers initially evaluated a world where every participant maintained a **BMI < 25**. 

However, they found this intervention to be **unrealistic** and **problematic**. For a patient with a baseline BMI of 35, forcing a drop to 25 within a single two-year period is an event rarely, if ever, seen in real-world data. When we ask a model to "off-road" into these extreme, unobserved scenarios, the resulting estimates lose their practical utility.

### The MTP Solution: The 5% Realistic Shift
Instead of forcing every patient toward an arbitrary "normal" weight, Taubman et al. suggested a much more clinically plausible alternative: **the decremental shift**. 

They proposed that a "more realistic intervention" would be to reduce BMI by a small percentage—such as **5%**—among all subjects with a BMI over 25. This is the essence of MTP logic: it respects the patient’s **actual starting point** and evaluates a change that a clinician could reasonably implement in a real-world clinic.

### A Lesson in Observational Complexity
Interestingly, the study’s estimate for the effect of the BMI intervention on CHD risk was **null**. The researchers noted this was likely due to the inherent complexities of observational data, such as **reverse causation** (where undiagnosed illness causes weight loss) and measurement error. 

For the clinician-researcher, this is a vital takeaway: MTPs provide a more realistic framework for intervention, but they still require high-quality data and a rigorous approach to confounding.

> **As a historical note:** While we use the name "MTP" today, Haneuse and Rotnitzky note that the concept was first introduced by Robins in 2004 and further developed here by Taubman et al., who applied it to both **BMI reduction** and **physical activity** (e.g., ensuring a "floor" of 30 minutes of daily exercise).

**Reference:**
* Taubman, S. L., Robins, J. M., Mittleman, M. A., & Hernán, M. A. (2009). Intervening on risk factors for coronary heart disease: an application of the parametric g-formula. *International Journal of Epidemiology*, 38(6), 1599-1611.

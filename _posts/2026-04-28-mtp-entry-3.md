In our previous entries, we **explained** how the framework of **Modified Treatment Policies (MTPs)** allows us to move beyond fixed targets. By using an intervention that is a function of the patient’s starting point—the "Natural Value"—we can ask questions that mirror real-world clinical shifts. 

While **Shift Interventions** (adding a fixed amount to everyone’s baseline) are the most common application of this framework, they are just one way to apply the MTP logic. To see why this matters, we turn to a highly impactful application: **Vaccine Efficacy and Immunobridging.**

### The Real-World Context: COVE and ENSEMBLE
The primary goal of major Phase 3 trials like **COVE (Moderna)** and **ENSEMBLE (Janssen)** was to assess the efficacy of the vaccine—a binary "yes/no" treatment—against COVID-19 infection. 

However, these trials also provided an opportunity to study **Correlates of Protection (CoP)**. By analyzing the vaccine arm, researchers wanted to learn the relationship between immune markers (like NAb titers) and the risk of infection. 

### Why Study the "Shift" in Titers?
This isn't just a statistical curiosity; it is fundamentally about validating **surrogate endpoints**. By modeling the NAb-infection relationship in the vaccine arm, we map out how the **population's risk of infection** directly responds to hypothetical, population-wide increases in antibody levels.

Why does this risk curve matter? Because infection risk is the building block for estimating **Vaccine Efficacy (VE)**. Once we use this curve to calculate the expected infection rate under a specific titer shift, we can combine that with placebo data to estimate the hypothetical VE of *any* intervention that elevates those antibodies. 

By validating the surrogate, this "Shift-Response Curve" translates biological markers into a roadmap for several distinct public health applications:
* **Dose Selection:** Helping define the optimal dose for a future vaccine by seeing exactly how much infection risk drops per unit of shift.
* **Booster Policy:** Deciding if a population requires a booster campaign based on the expected drop in infections.
* **Immunobridging:** Using early immune marker data to predict the final, long-term Vaccine Efficacy before a clinical trial concludes.

### The Utility of the Curve
By looking at the spectrum of shifts, we can identify where we get the most "bang for our buck." We might find that doubling antibody levels leads to a massive jump in protection, whereas a 10-fold increase offers only marginal extra benefit. This allows regulators and developers to set realistic, evidence-based targets for next-generation vaccines.

### A Subtle Technical Challenge: Unmeasured Confounding
As a researcher, I must acknowledge a subtlety: studying the relationship between NAbs and infection in the vaccine arm is essentially an **observational study**. Even though the trial was randomized, the specific antibody level a patient develops is not.

This means the analysis is subject to **unmeasured confounding**. For instance, a patient's underlying "immune capacity" might influence both their antibody response and their natural ability to fight off the virus. 

To handle this, sophisticated methods have been developed—including work I have been involved in regarding **Proximal Causal Inference for MTPs**. These methods leverage observable proxies of the underlying immune capacity to mitigate bias due to unmeasured confounding, yielding a Shift-Response Curve that more reliably isolates the effect of the neutralizing antibodies.

> **The Scientist’s Perspective:** "The vaccine example shows that MTPs are more than just a math exercise; they are a bridge that allows us to take data from today’s trials and use it to predict the success of tomorrow’s vaccines."

We’ve seen that **Modified Treatment Policies** allow us to evaluate realistic medical changes—like 15-minute surgical reductions or 5% weight loss. But as with any clinical evidence, the validity of these results depends on a few ground rules.

When we use an MTP, we are making three implicit promises about our data and our policy. If these rules are broken, our "causal" conclusions might just be statistical noise.

### Rule 1: The "Real-World" Check (Positivity)
In simple terms: **You cannot study what you haven’t seen.**

The policy must only "shift" patients to treatment levels that we actually observed in our data. Crucially, this must hold true for **every type of patient profile** (or "stratum") in the study. 

If we are shifting surgical times for 80-year-old patients with heart disease, we must actually have data on similar 80-year-olds who naturally had those shorter times. If the **Shifted Distribution** moves into a "ghost zone" where no **Observed Distribution** exists for that specific patient profile, we have no real-world evidence to learn from.

### Rule 2: The "Fair Comparison" Rule (Exchangeability)
To trust our results, we must ensure we are comparing apples to apples. This requires identifying and measuring all **confounders**. 

> **What is a Confounder?** A confounder is a factor—like age or disease severity—that influences **both** the treatment (surgery duration) and the outcome (the complication). If not accounted for, a confounder will "confuse" the investigator by distorting the true relationship. It can make an ineffective treatment look effective, or an unharmful one look harmful.

This is the "Big Ask" of all causal inference. Whether you are using MTPs or traditional methods, you are assuming there are **no hidden ghosts** influencing both the exposure and the outcome.

As we saw in our vaccine entry, this assumption is often under fire. Subject matter knowledge and clinical expertise are the only tools we have to decide if we’ve measured enough. If a major biological factor is missing, the "comparison" is no longer fair.

### Rule 3: The "No-Collapse" Rule (Smoothness)
So far, we have mostly discussed simple shifts (adding or subtracting a fixed amount). However, MTPs can be much more general. In our last entry, we saw a policy that only shifted people above a certain BMI threshold while doing nothing for those below it. As long as a policy is **smooth** and **invertible** (meaning we can "undo" the shift mathematically), it is generally valid.

The most common way this rule is broken is through a **Collapsible Policy**. A valid policy should not "crush" or "collapse" a whole range of different observed values into one single point (like forcing everyone with a BMI between 30 and 35 to be exactly 25). This creates a "Data Pile-up," making the statistical results unreliable.

We will see some clinical interventions that differ from strict fixed shifts in our next entry, but they all must respect this "No-Collapse" requirement to provide a valid result.
